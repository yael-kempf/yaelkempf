Yaël Kempf "Listen With Your Heart"

In her concluding exhibition titled "Listen With Your Heart" at the summer studio in Kulturbahnhof Eller, Yaël Kempf presents works that delve into the interdependence of humans and nature. It explores a balance that must not falter to safeguard existence. However, in reality, humans, in their nature, take possession and ultimately disrupt the harmony of shared lifelines.

Daily, we are bombarded with scientific data, satellite images, and climate charts that vividly illustrate the climate change and its devastating consequences for both humans and nature. Art, on the other hand, can operate completely free from this scientific objectivity and morality. It employs alternative methods to approach life in a seemingly diminishing world. The artist creates a unique framework for a sensory and aesthetic representation.
Yaël Kempf's artistic work is centered on this concept. Her works do not depict a gloomy apocalypse, nor do they serve as a warning finger. Instead, they are poetic and marked by a romanticized utopia of a better world.
In the exhibition, she works with plant-based materials, using Nori seaweed and rice paper for her forms, surfaces, and spatial installations. Dry algae leaves are dissolved in water and scooped with a sieve to be molded into new forms. The result is fragile images and vulnerable structures unfolding in space. Yaël incorporates plants from the surrounding area, gathered during walks around Kulturbahnhof, linking the interior and exterior spaces and providing the plants with a new space to grow.
Her spatial installations often incorporate sound or video. In the old Eller station, she allows music from the outside to permeate through the building's cracks and crevices, inviting visitors to listen through headphones in her spatial installation. The chosen song is "Listen With Your Heart" from the film Pocahontas. The myth of the story is the supposedly peaceful colonization of North America by Europeans, with the main character being the daughter of an Indian chief who mediates between Indians and the English.
"See things with the eye in your heart, not with the eye in your head." (John Fire Lame Deer, Sioux-Lakota)
Yaël Kempf's works aim to sharpen our perception of fragility, making us feel and see what has been lost.
Mara Sporn
